## 1. Purpose of Language

### What language is for

Language in Paritsea does one thing: it helps people **see what was already there but could not be named.**

Not explain. Not teach. Not persuade. See.

The role is to make the invisible legible — the hidden cost inside a functional system, the wound beneath a broken relationship, the structural gap inside an organization that looks successful. When something is named, it becomes workable. That is the only outcome language is responsible for.

### What language must never attempt

| Prohibited function | Why |
|--------------------|-----|
| Convince the reader of anything | Paritsea does not optimize for conversion or belief adoption |
| Make the reader feel urgency | Urgency is extractive; depth is pulled, never pushed |
| Explain what to do | Paritsea names what is happening, never prescribes next steps |
| Position itself against other systems | No comparison framing exists anywhere |
| Sell, promote, or build credential | The work is the authority; language does not claim authority |
| Inspire or motivate | Motivation is surface; Paritsea reads beneath it |
| Comfort the reader prematurely | Real naming sometimes discomforts before it clarifies |

## 2. Core Writing Principles

These principles are extracted directly from the existing Home, About, Journal, and Framework copy. They are not aspirational — they describe what already works.

### P-01 — Observation before conclusion

Every insight begins with what can be seen, not what can be claimed. The conclusion arrives after the observation, not instead of it.

> ✅ "Why do capable people burn out in organizations that look fine."
> ❌ "Organizations often fail their most talented employees."

The first names a visible thing. The second draws a conclusion without showing the observation.

### P-02 — Name before explain

Name the thing first. Once it has a name, it can be explained. Explanation without naming is noise.

> ✅ "It is to name what people feel but cannot say — and make it visible enough to work with."
> ❌ "Paritsea provides a framework for articulating complex emotional and organizational dynamics."

The second explains without naming. The first names the act.

### P-03 — The below-the-surface move

Every claim has a beneath layer. Paritsea's signature move is surfacing it. The writing pattern: state what is visible → pivot to what is beneath.

> ✅ "Paritsea doesn't read what's visible. It reads what's behind it — motivation, fear, wounds, and the tensions hidden inside a situation."

This is the core structural move. It appears throughout Home, About, and Journal.

### P-04 — Contrasting pair: NOT X, but Y

Paritsea's identity is built partly through negation. The "not X, but Y" structure appears frequently and is a deliberate tool for precision — not for dismissal, but to draw the exact boundary of what something is.

> ✅ "Not judgment. Understanding."
> ✅ "Not abstract. A method that surfaces hidden structures."
> ✅ "Not a perspective built from theory. But from observing people in real situations."

These pairs work because the first half is expected and the second half reframes. Never use this pattern for competitive positioning — only for precision of scope.

### P-05 — Specific over general, concrete over abstract

Abstract statements are only permitted when immediately grounded by a concrete example or image.

> ✅ "Sometimes we talk about dashboards. But really we're talking about 'people inside systems.'"
> ✅ "Sometimes the heaviest thing isn't a broken system or a failing organization. It's one person — lost, confused, carrying something that doesn't have a name yet."
> ❌ "Paritsea addresses both structural and human dimensions of complex situations."

The first two use the "sometimes" pattern — a specific entry into a universal insight. The third describes without showing.

### P-06 — Sentence length mirrors depth

Surface observations: short. Complex structural patterns: longer, with internal rhythm.

Hero-level sentences are short to punchy. Framework sentences can carry more weight because the reader is already deep. Never write a long sentence at the surface and a short one deep inside a Framework document.

### P-07 — First person is honest, not promotional

"I" and "ฉัน" are used in About. Not in Journal (which is analytical), not in Framework (which is authoritative). First person signals origin and context — never credentials or authority claims.

> ✅ "I draw on perspectives across multiple dimensions — psychology, systems, organizations, and human relationships — to read situations with depth and precision."
> ❌ "With years of expertise across psychology, systems, and organizational dynamics, I have developed..."

The second builds credential. The first describes a practice.

### P-08 — Unhurried rhythm; never rushed

Paritsea does not hurry the reader. Short sentences do not mean rushed. Brevity is precision, not speed.

Words that introduce urgency (`now`, `finally`, `before it's too late`, `don't miss`) do not appear anywhere.

### P-09 — Questions open, never close

Questions are used to open a section (name an unresolved pattern) and are never rhetorical deflections. They should contain the tension, not resolve it.

> ✅ "Why do relationships fall apart when no one is actually bad."
> ✅ "Why do things that look right feel wrong from the inside."
> ❌ "Have you ever wondered why you feel unfulfilled?"

The third is a hook. The first two are observations wearing the shape of a question.

### P-10 — The reader is never directly addressed as a student

Paritsea does not teach. It reads. The reader is not "you" who needs to learn something — the reader is a peer who is invited to see something.

> ✅ "The answers tend to live in the layer people don't often stop to look at."
> ❌ "In this article, you will learn three ways to identify hidden structural tension."

## 3. Tone Model

### Paritsea IS

| Quality | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Human and grounded** | Speaks like a thoughtful person, not an institution |
| **Reflective** | Considers before concluding; unhurried |

| **Honest about limits** | Names what is not known; does not claim more than is there |

| Register | Why it is absent |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Teaching register** | Paritsea does not give lessons. It names patterns. "You should" and "here's how" do not appear. |

| **Therapy language** | No "healing journey," no "safe space" positioning, no clinical diagnosis framing |

| **Guru register** | No revelatory framing ("what nobody tells you"), no insider positioning, no "I discovered" |
| **Marketing register** | No feature lists, no social proof, no conversion-optimized copy |

## 4. English Writing Rules

### Preferred sentence constructions

**Declarative + contrast pivot**
> "Paritsea doesn't read what's visible. It reads what's behind it."

**Observation + naming**
> "Relationships fall apart not because someone is bad, but because of wounds, fear, and things never understood."

**The 'sometimes' entry**
> "Sometimes the heaviest thing isn't a broken system or a failing organization. It's one person — lost, confused, carrying something that doesn't have a name yet."

**Named list with no preamble**
> "Motivation, fear, wounds, and the tensions hidden inside a situation."
(No "including the following:" or "such as:")

**Short paragraph after long paragraph**
> Long sentence establishing complexity, then: "Not judgment. Understanding."

**Em dash for clarification, not decoration**
> "a way of thinking that understands both the complexity of the world — and the depth of people"
(The dash extends; it does not interrupt.)

### Avoided sentence constructions

| Pattern | Example | Why avoided |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Passive authority claim | "It has been found that..." | Paritsea speaks from observation, not studies |
| Feature list | "Paritsea offers X, Y, and Z" | Product framing |
| Benefit statement | "This helps you feel more..." | Outcome-promising |
| Transition words as padding | "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In conclusion," | Paritsea does not summarize; it observes |
| Superlatives for authority | "The most effective way to..." | Comparative framing |
| Nominalizations | "the achievement of understanding" | Use verbs: "to understand" |
| "Journey" and "transformation" | "your growth journey" | Motivational register |
| "Empower," "leverage," "unlock" | Any marketing verb | Commercial register |

### Punctuation style

- **Em dash (—):** Clarification or extension. Not parenthetical interruption.
- **Period:** Sentence ends when the thought ends. Not earlier for effect (unless deliberate contrast pair).
- **Question mark:** Only for genuine questions used as section openers or to hold unresolved tension.
- **Exclamation:** Never.
- **Colon:** Introduces a list or names what follows. Used sparingly.
- **Ellipsis:** Not used. If a thought is incomplete, complete it.

## 5. Thai Writing Rules

### Core principle

Thai copy is not a translation of English copy. It is written for Thai readers using patterns natural to Thai thought, Thai rhythm, and Thai digital language. The meaning is preserved; the phrasing is not.

### Thai writing rules

**TH-01 — Start with what is NOT**
Thai readers find "ไม่ใช่ X แต่ Y" more natural than English "Not X, but Y" but the structure is the same. This is a primary Thai construction in Paritsea copy.
> `ไม่ใช่วิธีคิดที่เกิดจากทฤษฎี แต่จากการสังเกตผู้คนในสถานการณ์จริง`
> (Not a way of thinking from theory, but from observing people in real situations)

**TH-02 — Shorter clauses, more pauses**
Thai uses natural clause breaks where English would use subordinate clauses. Break what English chains into one long sentence.
> EN: "Not a perspective built from theory. But from observing people in real situations — closely, across many dimensions — until what others walk past becomes visible."
> TH: `ไม่ใช่วิธีคิดที่เกิดจากทฤษฎี แต่จากการสังเกตผู้คนในสถานการณ์จริงอย่างละเอียดและหลายมิติ จนเห็นสิ่งที่คนอื่นมักผ่านไป`

**TH-03 — Clarity before sophistication**
If a Thai term requires reader effort, use a simpler term. Thai elegance is in clarity, not vocabulary complexity.
> Use: `การนำไปปรับใช้จริง` (Implementation)
> Not: `การประยุกต์ใช้` (deprecated — too abstract)
> Never: `แก่น` (banned — archaic, wrong altitude for nav)

**TH-04 — English nouns accepted in Thai copy**
Proper nouns, system terms, and English vocabulary that has no natural Thai equivalent are used directly. Thai verbs precede them naturally.
> ✅ `อ่าน Framework` → natural (Thai verb + English noun)
> ✅ `สำรวจ Journal` → natural
> ✅ `Protocol มาจาก Framework` → natural in system context
> ✅ `ไม่ใช่ abstract` → natural in descriptive context

**TH-05 — Thai should not carry more weight than English**
If the Thai version sounds more philosophical or poetic than the English version, it is over-written. Both versions should feel equivalent in register.

**TH-06 — Language parity rule**
Thai and English must carry identical altitude. A Thai version of a Journal entry must feel observational, not prescriptive. A Thai Framework document must feel authoritative, not lyrical.

| English | Correct Thai | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| sees / reads | `อ่าน` (reads) | Paritsea's lens verb — use `อ่าน` over `มองเห็น` |

## 7. UX Copy Rules

### Navigation labels

- Always English, both TH and EN locale (D-029)
- Single words or proper nouns only: `Journal` `System` `Framework` `Protocols` `Standards` `Implementation` `About`
- No articles, no prepositions, no descriptors
- ❌ Never: "The Journal," "System Hub," "View All Protocols"

### CTA buttons

**Rules:**
- Verb + Object only. No filler words.
- Maximum 3 words
- No urgency words (`Now`, `Today`, `Don't miss`)
- No benefit promises (`Start transforming`, `Unlock access`)

| ✅ Correct | ❌ Wrong |
|-----------|---------|
| Read Framework | Start Your Journey |
| Explore Journal | Unlock Deep Thinking |
| View Implementations | Learn More Now |
| อ่าน Framework | เริ่มต้นการเดินทางของคุณ |

| ดูการนำไปปรับใช้จริง | ค้นพบ Framework ของคุณ |

| Journal post | None above fold; lateral links at tail |

### Section headings

- Descriptive, not imperative
- No "How to," "Top X," "Why you should"

### Page introductions (first visible text after title)

- 1–2 sentences maximum at heading level
- States what the layer IS, not what it does for the reader
- Does not start with "Welcome to" or "This page contains"

## 10. Rewrite Evaluation Checklist

Before approving any rewritten or newly written copy for Paritsea, this checklist must pass. Any NO is a rejection.

### A. Altitude Check

- [ ] Does this piece answer exactly ONE of the questions its page is permitted to answer?
- [ ] Is the writing register correct for this layer? (Journal = observational; Framework = authoritative; etc.)
